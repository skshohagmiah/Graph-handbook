Chapter 1

What Are Graphs, Really?

"A graph is not just a mathematical abstraction‚Äîit's a lens for understanding the connected world around us."

Introduction

Every day, you interact with graphs without even knowing it:

  • When Facebook suggests friends, it's analyzing your social graph
  • When Google Maps finds the fastest route, it's traversing a road graph
  • When Netflix recommends movies, it's using a preference graph
  • When you browse the internet, you're navigating a web graph
Life Insight: Your relationships, goals, and daily decisions all form invisible graphs. Understanding this structure helps you navigate social networks, career paths, and life choices more effectively.

What Is a Graph?

A graph is simply a collection of:

  • Nodes (or Vertices): The "things" in your system
  • Edges: The connections between those things

Graph Components

Nodes (Vertices)

  • The fundamental units of a graph
  • Can represent anything: people, cities, web pages, concepts
  • Usually labeled with identifiers (0, 1, 2... or "Alice", "Bob", "Charlie")

Edges

  • Connections between nodes
  • Can be directed (one-way) or undirected (two-way)
  • May have weights (costs, distances, strengths)

Degree

  • Degree of a node: Number of edges connected to it
  • In directed graphs:
    • In-degree: Number of incoming edges
    • Out-degree: Number of outgoing edges

Types of Graphs

1. Directed vs Undirected

Undirected Graph: Edges have no direction

  • Example: Facebook friendships (mutual)
  • If A is friends with B, then B is friends with A

Directed Graph (Digraph): Edges have direction

  • Example: Twitter follows (one-way)
  • A can follow B without B following A back

2. Weighted vs Unweighted

Unweighted Graph: All edges are equal

  • Example: Simple friendship network

Weighted Graph: Edges have values (weights)

  • Example: Road network with distances
  • Example: Social network with relationship strength
Exercise: Map Your Social Circle
  1. Put yourself in the center
  2. Add your closest 5-6 friends as nodes
  3. Draw edges between people who know each other

Key Takeaways

  • Graphs are everywhere - social networks, maps, internet, knowledge systems
  • Simple concept - just nodes (things) connected by edges (relationships)
  • Two main types - directed/undirected, weighted/unweighted
  • Real-world graphs are usually sparse - most things aren't connected to everything
  • Graph thinking is systems thinking - see relationships, not just individual entities
Life Application: Start seeing your world as graphs: your career network, learning dependencies, daily routines, decision trees. Understanding these structures gives you power to optimize and navigate them more effectively.
